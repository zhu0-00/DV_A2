White-Hat: This visualization uses OECD greenhouse gas inventory data to show how emissions change over time and what sectors make up emissions for a selected country.



Black-Hat: This visualization uses the same dataset but employs a logarithmic scale for the y-axis while keeping the axis labels in linear scale, which can mislead viewers about the relative differences in emissions between countries.

Assignment Write-Up

White-Hat

This visualization uses the OECD dataset Air Emissions - Greenhouse Gas Emissions Inventories, sourced from National Inventory Submissions 2025 to the UNFCCC. The dataset includes annual, human-caused greenhouse gas emissions across countries, pollutants, and source categories. It reports emissions in CO₂-equivalent units and includes categories such as energy, agriculture, waste, and industrial processes.

For our design, we used two views. First, a line chart shows how emissions changed over time for the five largest emitters in the latest year, because line charts are effective for accurately showing trends. Second, a sector breakdown bar chart shows which source categories contribute most to emissions in one selected country (which the user selects from a dropdown). There is filtering present due to the drop-down, and we perform aggregation to determine country-year totals and sector-totals (which then sort the top countries). We also implemented tooltips for the second graph.

This page is designed to be easy to interpret for a general audience. It explicitly highlights the filtering at the top so it is clear what is happening on the page and the user has autonomy over what they see. The visualization also avoids distorted scales and misleading color maps, as everything is scaled in a standard, consistent way, and the colors are easy to differentiate. There are clear captions to explain what each graph shows and all the axes are labeled thoroughly. For the sector graph, the user can hover on the bars to see the exact numbers if they want more detailed information. Finally, for both the line chart and bar chart, sizes and distances between the lines/bars are kept consistent so that it is easy to make accurate comparisons between different countries.

Black-Hat

In our Black Hat visualization we implemented the following designs so that the major countries appear to be not blameworthy for greenhouse gas emission.

Each country's proportion contribution is misrepresented by a logarithmic transformation of the y axis scale and an area overlay rather than a cumulative stacking of the total emission. The countries are not stacked properly but stacked with overlay so only the area difference is shown. So the apparent area is not proportional to the absolute emission contribution.

The overview color scheme is misleading and counterintuitive, the intensity of the color decreases the emission contribution increases, suggesting the viewers into thinking that the minor countries are contributing more greenhouse gas. Together with the log scale and the color scheme, the colored apparent area for all of the countries looks similar overall. The relative sizes of the areas are imposed on the viewers such that they consider that to be the relative contribution among the countries. One of the effect of these techniques is that the US, the biggest contributor having a even smaller apparent area than Algeria. The illusion that an country's apparent area is representing its total emission is reinforced by zooming in on each country. When the viewers click on a area, a breakdown by the categories is overlaid on the area window only rather than the entire area under the trendline.

The zoom effect also brings the max value of a country to the ceiling of the graph, the smallest contributor will have the biggest zoom, covering the entire svg area; whereas for example the US since already at the top of the chart is barely zoomed. In addition, the y-axis is hidden upon zooming so that the viewer's attention is forced onto relative areas rather than the true numerical scale.

Only when the viewers zoom in on each area and read the tool tips showing the absolute emission values they can perhaps finally notice the deception; but even so, the percentage breakdowns are overshadowing the true numerical values in parentheses.
